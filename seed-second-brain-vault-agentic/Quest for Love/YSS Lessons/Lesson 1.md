# The Way to [[Self-realisation]]

### Expanding Your Consciousness

The way to a higher life is through the gradual expansion of your consciousness from delusory identification with your human nature and limitations to the perception and expression of your inherent divinity. 

Life becomes every more beautiful - richer and more wonderful in every way - as you progress step by step to higher and more refined states of consciousness, and thereby see through the coverings of matter and gross vibrations that obscure the glory of the indwelling Spirit within you and pervading all creation.

The most elevated states of consciousness lead at last to [[Self-realisation]]: our native state as the immortal soul, the pure reflection of the ever-perfect, ever-blissful Spirit, with its awakened faculties of divine perception. That is the state of freedom and joy you should strive wholeheartedly to achieve.  ^9e5a48

### Always Remember What [[Self-realisation]] means

As you pursue the studies and spiritual practices in these Lessons, keep the goal always before you, and the definition of [[Self-realisation]] that you have been give:

**[[Self-realisation|Self-realization]] is the knowing - in body, mind and soul - that we are one with the omnipresence of God; that we do not have to pray that it come to us, that we are not merely near it at all times, but that God's omnipresence is our omnipresence; that we are just as much a part of Him now as we every will be. All we have to do is improve our knowing.** ^7f7ab7

### Yoga: The Way to [[Self-realisation]]

Great spiritual teachers of India long ago discovered a psycho-physiological method for achieving conscious contact with the Divine Presence. They learned the science of reuniting the individualized soul-wave with the sea of Spirit. This method, _Raja Yoga_, has been used by Hindu sages for many thousands of years. In the Bhagavad Gita Bhagavan Krishna says: "The yogi is deemed greater that body-disciplining ascetics, greater even than the followers of the path of wisdom or the path of action; be thou, O Arjuna, a yogi!"[^1]

As an individualised self, the soul has descended from the universality of Spirit and become identified with the limitations of the body and its sense-consciousness. Through _maya_ or delusion, the changeless immortal soul becomes subjectively identified with change and mortality - imagining itself to be painfully separated from its Eternal Source.

The word _yoga_ means "union": first of the limited human ego with the ever-perfect soul, the divine essence residing in every person; and then of the soul with God. The word _yoga_ also refers to the physical, mental, and spiritual practices that bestow that conscious contact with the Infinite Source of all creation - practices that gradually enable the progressing yogi to attain self-mastery in the following ways:

1. bringing the body under the conscious control of the higher faculties of the concentrated mind, will and discriminative wisdom;
2. bringing the untrained and misguided mental faculties under benign rulership of the true "emperor" of the bodily kingdom: the soul or real Self (image of God in man), with its inherent qualities of divine wisdom, love, and bliss-consciousness;
3. uniting the individual Self (the soul dwelling in the finite bodily house) with the infinite, omnipresent Spirit pervading all creation and beyond.

## How the Soul Became Trapped in the Body, and How to Free it

Yoga is a vast and intricate science. This profound science will be fully unfolded as you progress in your study of the _YSS Lessons_ and practice of the meditation techniques. To begin your journey toward spiritual freedom and bliss, it is sufficient to keep in mind the following simplified explanation, which will prepare you for practice of the initial techniques of [[Self-realisation]] taught later in this Lesson. 

### The Soul and Its Three Bodies

Man, in essence, is a soul - a radiating ray of the infinite Universal Spirit.

Each soul-ray takes on individuality by embodying itself - first in a causal body made of bliss and pure intuitive consciousness; then in an astral body composed of mind and _prana_, indestructible light and life energy; which in turn is covered with a physical body of perishable matter.

Remember these truths as you gaze upon your mortal form. Hidden within your familiar frame of flesh and bones is a wondrous astral body of light and divine life. And that astral from is itself only a cloak for your causal body, the superfine idea-body or individualised matrix of creative consciousness in which the soul - spark of divinity within you - is manifesting.

The native consciousness of the soul is one with God's cosmic consciousness. As the soul descends successively into each of the three bodily vehicles - causal, astral, and physical - and becomes identified with these vehicles' progressively decreased faculties and instruments of perception, its infinite consciousness, wisdom, and bliss become ever more constrained by delusion and ignorance (_maya_ and avidya).

### Soul Versus Ego

The soul, identifying with the physical body and its imperfect instruments of sensory perception, becomes the human ego. The purpose of all true spiritual teachings is to enable each individual to transcend the ego and its multifarious flawed attitudes and attachments, so that all may regain the lost paradise of soul consciousness and know that they are, and every have been, one with Spirit. 

The most direct means of achieving that transcendence and freedom is meditation.

### The Spiritual Eye

From the very outset of your practice of meditation, it is important to know about the powerful centre of divine consciousness located at the point between the eyebrows. This is the seat of the spiritual eye, the [[Christ Consciousness]] centre (Kutastha), the "door" or "single eye" spoken of by [[Jesus]]; the subtle centre of will and intuitive perception in the body.[^2]

The soul, encased in its two finer bodies of though and life energy, actually enters the physical body at the time of conception through primal seed-cell formed by the united sperm and ovum, which is the first vestige of the medulla oblongata at the base of the brain. The spiritual eye is the visible reflection of the subtle spiritual centre (ajna chakra_) in the medulla. 

The spiritual eye is the portal to soul consciousness. The ever blissful soul abides in the regions of elevated spiritual consciousness accessed by opening and passing through the starry door of the spiritual eye. Indeed, the five-pointed silvery-white star in the spiritual eye is the very source of the astral forces that have materialised into your star-shaped human body (the head being the top point of the star and the arms and legs its four other points). By will power, those astral energies flowing from the spiritual eye can be consciously employed to recharge the body and all its parts with divine light and vitality.

The thrilling mysteries of the spiritual eye and its divine states of consciousness will be elaborated in later Lessons. For now, know that concentration on and awareness of this centre is the prerequisite for meditation - for lifting the consciousness and life energy from body- identification to soul awakening, as will be taught below. 

### Life Energy Is the Link Between Consciousness and Matter

The yoga science of meditation is based on the truth that life force (cosmic energy or prana) is the missing link between consciousness and matter. As long as one is alive and breathing, consciousness along with the life force flows down and outward - emanating from the astral centres secreted within the central nervous system of the brain and spine (the _Chakras_) - into the nerves, muscles, organs, and sensory instruments of the physical body, By this intricate process, the life force and the breath tie the consciousness to the physical form.[^3]

In that state of body identification, the mind is limited by what it can perceive through the physical senses of sight, hearing, touch, taste, and smell. Attaching itself to the body and material world, which are constantly changing, the mind is subject to constant disturbance by restlessness, fluctuating feelings and emotions, and excitation of desires.

Meditation is the art and technique of overcoming that identification so that the soul can abide in the perfect calmness and tranquility of its true nature[^4] - temporarily at first, and then with increasingly enduring stability as the yogi progresses.

An initial step in attaining the transcendence bestowed by yoga, therefore, is becoming aware of the life energy as distinct from and independent of the physical body. By practice of the [[Yogoda Satsanga]] Technique of Energisation [to be introduced in the next Lesson and fully taught in Lesson 6], one can definitely feel the difference between the physical flesh and non-physical life energy that sustains it. 

Deeper and more extensive explanation of the above concepts will be given as you progress in your study. However, as with all spiritual instruction, it is vital to assimilate what you learn - not just with the mind but with the whole being, through actual practice - before trying to cram the mind with further knowledge. Take time now to crystallise your understanding of the truths introduced above by incorporating practice of the following techniques into your daily routine. 

# Learning to Meditate: Basic Instructions

Meditation is the prime requisite of a successful search for God. All other spiritual injunctions are subordinate, their practice contributing to the efficacy of the all-important meditative effort. Only meditation, which stills the body and mind and frees the attention to focus on God, enables the seeker to realise the indwelling, omnipresent Spirit.

The following preliminary instructions in meditation will enable you to begin today to master this divine art.

## Where to Meditate

As the living room produces conversation consciousness; the bathroom, cleansing consciousness; the bedroom, sleeping consciousness; the kitchen, food consciousness, so also a little place used only for meditation produces silence or peace consciousness. A quiet, evenly heater room, neither too warm nor cold; a corner screened off; a well-ventilated closet; or a secluded place outdoors in nature: any of these is suitable for meditation.

If possible, select a quiet place and establish there your meditation "temple". Many find that meditation before an altar inspires devotion. Students of [[Yogoda Satsanga Society of India]] may place on their altar pictures of [[Bhagavan Krishna]], [[Jesus]] Christ, and the line of [[Yogoda Satsanga]] Gurus - [[Mahavatar Babaji]], [[Lahiri Mahasaya]], [[Swami Sri Yukteswarji]], and your Guru [[Paramhansa Yogananda]] - who have founded this path to God, and who eternally bless and assist those who follow it. These pictures may be placed on a wall in the area where one meditates, or on a small table or shelf. [See "Handbook and Glossary for Students of the [[YSS Lessons]]"]

Your meditation seat should face the altar, if you have one, and preferably the east.[^5] If you like to sit on the floor, cross-legged or in traditional Indian posture of _[[Padmasana]]_ (the lotus pose), and you are supple enough to do so, you may choose for your meditation seta a cushion or a small rug or mat. Or you may with equal benefit sit on an armless straight chair. Place over the meditation seat a woollen blanket or cloth that covers the entire seat and runs down under the feet. This will help insulate your body from earth currents, so that their opposite magnetic pull will not impede the flow of the life current and consciousness which you are trying to draw upward through the spinal centres to the higher centres of divine consciousness in the brain. The wool may be covered with a silk cloth for further insulation, if you wish. 

## When to Meditate 

Practise meditation in the early morning and before going to bed at night, when the following pronounced changes that occur in the body can be used to advantage: In the morning, the body is calm and refreshed by sleep, and by its nightlong fast from food and conscious mental and physical activity; residual restlessness-producing waste products from food ingested the previous day are eliminated; the body assumes the wakeful active state uniquely prepared for meditation. At night the body again is relaxed, the mind is free from the the day's duties, and the life force can easily be withdrawn from the sensory nerves to the brain and redirected toward God.

Don't drug yourself with too much sleep, and thus lose your vitality. Six hours of sleep is sufficient for most adults who practise deep yoga meditation. Sunrise is an auspicious and beneficial time for meditation; your home and neighbourhood usually are quiet at this time. Metaphysically, this hour is advantageous because the rays and vibrations of the dawn are vitalising and spiritually uplifting. Similarly, the hour of the sunset is a good time for meditation. If you cannot practise then, choose your evening meditation period between the hours of nine o'clock and midnight. When most people are quiet and asleep, your remain awake in God. 

Dawn, noon, sunset, and midnight correspond with the four seasons: dawn with spring, noon with summer, sunset with fall, and midnight with winter. The daily "seasons" cause bodily changes. The practice of meditation during these changing cycles of the day can have revitalising effects, physically, mentally and spiritually.[^6]

In addition to your regular periods of meditation, find extra moments to interiorise the mind and turn your thoughts to God and to the inner calmness of meditation. If in those free moments you are not alone - for example, when you are riding on a bus or a plane, or even while sharing the same bedroom with your husband or wife - simply close your eyes and appear to be asleep, but enter the meditative state instead.

## Meditation Posture

One of the first requisites for effective meditation is correct posture. 

Those whose legs are limber may prefer to meditate sitting on the floor, or on a firm bed, in [[Padmasana]] or a simple cross-legged position. (See Illustrations [[IMG_1002 1-1.jpg|1]] and [[IMG_1002 1-1.jpg|2]] .) However, the following comfortable meditation posture is also recommended:

1. Sit on an armless straight chair (prepared as previously instructed). The chair should be of comfortable height; otherwise there is a tendency to curve the torso forward or backward. Rest your feet flat on the floor. 
2. Hold your spine, erect, abdomen drawn in comfortably, chest out, shoulders back, but without undue tension, and chin parallel to the ground. Do not lean against the back of the chair.
3. With palms upturned, rest each hand on the corresponding thigh where it joins the abdominal region, to help prevent the body from bending forward. This position of the hands also helps you to keep the shoulders back and the chest out. (See Illustrations [[IMG_1002 1-1.jpg|3]].)

![[IMG_1002 1-1.jpg]]

If you have assumed the correct posture, your body will be stable, yet relaxed, so that it is possible to remain completely still, without moving a muscle. Such stillness, devoid of restless body movements and adjustments, is essential to the attainment of a deep meditative state.

Each detail of the foregoing posture instructions contributes toward the major goal: an erect spine, held stable without strain or tension, for the duration of the meditation period. These instructions, except for the positions of the legs and feet, apply also to the cross-legged meditation pose. If, because of postural handicap or bodily defects, you experience difficulty or discomfort in attempting specified positions of the feet, hands, shoulders, chest or abdomen, make whatever adjustments are necessary to your personal needs, but strive always to accomplish the ideal posture. 

Strive steadily toward the ideal of remaining totally still during meditation, and of sustaining the correct posture without strain or tension. Restless movements of the body completely waste the calming benefits of one's meditative efforts. Improper posture during meditation causes restlessness, tension, and sleepiness. 

Check your posture periodically throughout your practice of meditation until you have firmly established the correct postural habits. If you feel any undue strain or tension in any part of the body, try consciously to relax it. 

## Eyes Focused at the Concentration Centre

During meditation the eyes should be turned upward, with the gaze and attention focused as though looking out through a point between the eyebrows. (See Illustration [[IMG_1003-1.jpg|4]].) This is the spiritual centre of concentration in the body. Note how persons deep in concentration often "knit" their brows, thus unconsciously focusing their attention at this centre. In meditation, the eyelids may be half-closed, or completely closed if this seems more comfortable to you. Do not cross the eyes or strain them; the upward gaze comes easily and naturally when one is relaxed and calmly concentrated. _Most important: fix the mind and the attention at the point between the eyebrows._[^7]

This is the [[Kutastha Chaitanya]] or Christ Consciousness centre, the seat of the single eye spoken of by Christ: "The light of the body is the eye: if therefore thine eye be single, thy whole body shall be full of light."[^8]

When the purpose of meditation is fulfilled, you find your consciousness automatically concentrated at the spiritual eye, and you experience, according to your inner spiritual capacity, a state of joyous divine union with Spirit.

## General Meditation Routine

### Preliminary Breathing Exercise 1

When you are established in the meditation pose just described, the next preparation for meditation is to rid the lungs of accumulated carbon dioxide, which causes restlessness. Expel the breath through the mouth in a double exhalation: "huh, huhhh." (This sound is made with the breath only, not the voice.) Then inhale deeply through the nostrils, tensing the whole body and clenching the fists. Hold the breath and tension to a count of six.[^9] Expel the breath through the mouth in a double exhalation, "huh, huhhh," and relax the tension. Repeat this three times. Tensing and relaxing the muscles in this way helps to withdraw the life force from body identification and makes it much easier to life the mind above material consciousness. 

### Prayer

With palms placed together reverently, offer God a prayer from your heart, expressing your devotion and asking His blessings on your meditation. At the conclusion of your prayer, _pranam_ toward the altar.[^10]

### Chanting

If you wish, you may chant mentally or aloud one of the [[Cosmic Chants]] to help awaken your devotion and concentrate the thoughts on God.[^11] (If chanting aloud, you may wish to play the chant on a harmonium or sing along with one of the YSS recordings.)

### Preliminary Breathing Exercise 2

Next, inhale slowly and deeply through the nostrils to a count of twenty; hold the breath to a count of twenty; exhale slowly and completely through the mouth to a count of twenty. Begin the next inhalation after no more than a comfortable pause. Practise this six to twelve times. (No uncomfortable pressure should be felt in the lungs. A shorter count may be used, provided this count is identical in each of the three parts.) With your final exhalation, forget the breath; let it flow in and out naturally of its own accord, as in ordinary breathing.

### What to Do During Meditation

Now you are ready to begin meditation, the concentration of your attention and consciousness toward communion with, and oneness with, God. In these _Lessons_ you will be offered various special techniques to deepen your concentration and direct your attention and consciousness toward God. In preparation for receiving these techniques, which will be taught one by one in these _Lessons_, you should begin now to train your body and mind in the spiritual discipline of meditation. The first step is to accustom yourself to sitting quietly in the meditation posture, with the attention concentrated at the point between the eyebrows, as previously described. Along with this, practise prayer or affirmation according to the following instructions. 

1. You may pray deeply in the language of your heart to God and His saints. Or simply call to God over and over again, with deeper and deeper longing and concentration: "Reveal Thyself! Reveal Thyself!" Feel that He is listening to your prayer, right where you are; for being omnipresent, God is with you always - as Peace, Joy, Light, or your own Infinite Beloved.
2. Students of [[Yogoda Satsanga Society of India]] also find it highly beneficial to pray to and invoke the presence and blessings of [[Bhagavan Krishna]], [[Jesus]] Christ, and the [[Yogoda Satsanga]] line of Gurus. As you spiritual guides, they will help you toward your goal of Self-unfoldment and realisation of God. A helpful prayer of this type is given on page 25. 
3. Another meditation practice is to select a prayer or other inspirational thought from the _Lessons_, or from [[Metaphysical Meditations]] or [[Whispers from Eternity]], and permeate it with your own devotional yearning. For example:

	_"Come Thou, O Father, reveal the vast kingdom of Thy presence! Reveal Thyself! Teach my heart to pray; teach my soul to feel that any moment all doors may open and Thy presence be revealed."
	
	 _"Teach me to feel that I am enveloped always in the aureole of Thine all-protecting omnipresence, in birth, in sorrow, in joy, in activity, in meditation, in ignorance, in trials, in death, in final emancipation."

Repeat your prayer over and over with devotion; meditate upon its meaning , until it becomes a part of your own realisation or until you feel God's response. 

4. Practising affirmation is an excellent meditation exercise. An affirmation is the statement of a truth that you wish to realise and manifest in your life. Examples of affirmations useful in meditation are:

	_"I am _submerged in eternal light. It permeates every particle of my being. I am living in that light. The Divine Spirit fills me within and without."_
	
	_"I relax and cast aside all mental burdens, allowing God to express through me His perfect love, peace, and wisdom."_

An affirmation should be repeated aloud (but not loudly) or mentally several times in succession during meditation, as well as several times during the day, with deep concentration and faith in its materialisation, until it manifests as a reality in your life. ([[Lesson 5]] discusses the metaphysical principles underlying the art of affirmation. This important spiritual technique is also described by [[Paramhansa Yogananda]] in his book [[Scientific Healing Affirmations]], which contains affirmations for a variety of purposes.)

While practising affirmation or praying to God, remember to keep the gaze and the consciousness centred at the point between the eyebrows. Each time you feel your attention wandering from the object or thought on which it is concentrating, gently bring it back. Meditate until you feel God's response: a calm, deep peace and inner joy. Peace is the first proof of God's presence within you.

### Length of Meditation

For the beginner, meditation should last at least fifteen to thirty minutes in the morning and fifteen to thirty minutes at night. The longer and deeper you meditate, the faster you will progress spiritually.

### Closing Prayer

End your meditation with a heartfelt prayer to God. _(Additional material, including video and audio instructions and guided meditations, can be found on the [[Yogoda Satsanga Society of India]] website, in the section, "**Learn to Meditate.**")_

## Points to Remember about Meditation

1. Some people fall asleep during meditation. You don't want that. You must be wide awake. If you have a spell of sleepiness, shake it off; briefly tense the body all over several times and sleep will disappear. Fresh air helps to keep one alert and awake, too.
2. It is essential that you sit upright. This is the first step toward successfully practising the techniques. When you sit with your spine bent and you lean over, what do you immediately think? That you are ready to give up. You must sit as if you mean business. In India we had to practise that for months before we were permitted to receive any spiritual instruction. 

_A bent spine is an enemy of [[Self-realisation]]._ Your mind will be on the bent spine; you will be muscle-bound. The electric currents of your life force will be busy with the muscles and flesh, and you will be unable to reverse its flow to concentrate upon God. Practise this discipline over the body, and you mind will be free to lift you consciousness from the body to the Infinite.

3. Be regular in your meditations (whether they be deep or, in the beginning, restless). Achievement in meditation as any other skill lies in continued regularity of activity. Regularity helps you form the habit of meditating; and habit makes the performance of any action easier. 
4. depth of concentration is more important than length of meditation, but both are necessary for advanced spiritual development.

Be persistent if you would attract God's attention. Remember: meditation must be practised with devotion, with love for God. Love for God means _craving_ for God, the magnetic attraction of love He cannot resist. Meditation techniques help kindle in you the divine craving that will take you to the heights of realisation and God-Bliss. Resolve that you will never give up until He comes to you. Even if you are not fully aware of His response, know that God is listening to the cry of your heart. In His own time He will give you the supreme gift of Himself.

## Spiritualising Your Consciousness in Daily Life

### Yoga is a Way of Life

Yoga is primarily as science of meditation: concentration on God and absorption of one's consciousness in Him. But as a natural prerequisite to the ability to meditate uninterruptedly on God, yoga also inculcates discipline and proper direction to spiritualise every aspect of human nature and daily life. 

For example. yoga enables one to solve all problems by sould guidance, the wisdom born of [[Self-realisation]]. So long as problems occupy the mind, there is little opportunity for mental and spiritual freedom, for these cannot coexist with mental turmoil. The mind should be cleared of confusing moods, worries, and fears so that in the sky of mental calmness the liberated bird of parasdise, the soul, can soar and spread its wings over one's whole existence. By gradually easing the perplexities and difficulties that burden one's life from within and without, yoga paves the way to freedom.

### You Can Experience the Same Divine Consciousness as the Masters

Ultimate freedom comes with the attainment of [[Self-realisation]]. One who has attained [[Self-realisation]] enjoys the same freedom, the same divine consciousness and qualities, that found perfect expression in avatars (divine incarnations) such as [[Bhagavan Krishna]] and [[Jesus]]. 

The words "Krishna" and "Christ" refer not only to two divine personages who lived on earth and brought great spiritual teachings for the upliftment of humanity, but also to a state of consciousness. They are titles connoting the omnipresent consciousness manifested. by these avatars and by other God-realised souls, such as my guru and _paramgurus[^12]_ - [[Swami Sri Yukteswarji]], [[Lahiri Mahasaya]], and [[Mahavatar Babaji]]. 

That omnipresent consciousness is the [[Kutastha Chaitanya]] (Krishna or Christ Consciousness), the projected consciousness and intelligence of God immanent in all creation. Through attainment of the Christ, or [[Kutastha]], Consciousness, you too may experience the universal realisation expressed by Jesus when he said, "I and my Father are one."[^13] St. John spoke of this consciousness in Jesus when he declared, "But as many as received him (the Christ Consciousness), to them gave he power to becomes the sons of God."[^14]

### Harmonising the Material and Spiritual Duties of Life

The [[Yogoda Satsanga Lessons]] teach the student not only how to gain personal realisation of the infinite treasures within, but also how to harmonise all of life's duties. The [[Yogoda Satsanga]] yoga teachings can be applied to every phase of life: to business, social, physical, mental, and spiritual development. Such all-round application of spiritual truths produces the highest bliss. 

One who desires to follow successfully the path of truth should live a life that is materially and spiritually harmonious. Material activities related to your physical body, family life, and producing income should be performed carefully and with conscientious effort. Your body should be kept healthy, your financial status sound, your matrimonial and social relationships harmonious, and your mental state well balanced. Your spiritual development should enable you to harmonise all your activities and duties so they contribute to your lasting peace and happiness.

Spiritual consciousness does not necessitate renouncing material possessions proper care of the body; it means spiritualising one's life, thoughts, and actions by bringing them into attunement with God and Truth. Yoga teaches you how to transmute matter-consciousness and body-consciousness into God-consciousness - how to train your mind so that you can perform the necessary dutiful actions of daily life and still maintain the consciousness of God within. 

Spiritual aspirants should not seek to flee their rightful responsibilities; fastest progress comes by performing one's spiritual _and_ material duties, without letting either crowd out the other. True renunciation means to renounce material consciousness for spiritual consciousness. The yoga term for this liberating attitude is nonattachment. Thus, the [[Bhagavad Gita]] teaches: "Always perform good material actions (karyam) and spiritual actions (karman) without attachment. By doing all actions without attachment, one attains the highest." [^15]

By teaching you the techniques whereby you may live in the joyous consciousness of the presence of God, nonattached to matter's false securities, yoga frees you from the pangs of material disappointments, and enables you to enjoy a happier, more fruitful and meaningful life. It bestows a deep inner state of fulfilment, which you automatically want to share in willing service to others.

### Necessary Necessities and Unnecessary Necessities

God-communion is the first, middle and last step of YSS teachings - ecstatic communion with God by morning and nightly meditation; and working for God during the day. The student of these _Lessons_ learns to perceive the Divine in the temple of meditation as well as in the temple of activity.

In the _YSS Lessons_ you are taught all the necessary techniques for perfecting your body, mind, and soul. But YSS also warns you not to specialise solely in health, or diet, or mental culture, or any other bypath to self-improvement; but to specialise first and foremost in communing with the Infinite Bliss of Spirit, which can forever end your miseries.

Most persons waste their precious time because they do not differentiate between their needs and their wants. This of the countless desires that have sunk roots in your brain and are made to seem "necessary" by the deluding influence of your materialistic worldly environment. Why waste your time in pursuing those unnecessary "necessities" that cannot really satisfy you and inevitably bring disillusionment? Wouldn't you rather use your time in seeking the truly necessary necessities of life - peace, health, real blessedness? You can do so by scheduling your daily life according to your highest spiritual necessity: your engagement, through meditation, with the God-Bliss within you. 

## How to Ensure Results From Your Spiritual Efforts

### Using the Power of Good Habits

To realise spiritual truths in your own consciousness, you must devote yourself to regular, continuing effort. Most people consume their leisure time in daydreaming, in the countless diversion of the modern world, or in nonproductive forms of reading, rather than in meaningful creative activity. As a result, they suffer from limitations of various kinds.

If you want to reap the richest possible harvest of truth in this short span of life, you should faithfully make these practical instructions a part of your every day. Each student should regularly and conscientiously, at whatever time of the day or night is convenient, devote a half hour to the study of the _Lessons_ (in addition to meditation). Make note of and analyse the essential for [[Self-realisation]] presented in the [[Lessons]] - and rearrange the pattern of you life accordingly. This is the way to prove to yourself the benefit of these teachings.

Do not put off study and meditation until tomorrow. That "tomorrow" will be ever-receding; procrastination and useless activities will swallow the present precious moments that might be used to bring you nearer to immortal achievement. By adapting your life to these truths you will more and more clearly understand the basic principles of true religion. Postponement of this effort not only delays your progress toward [[Self-realisation]], happiness, and inner peace, but leads to spirit-withering stagnation. 

It is a psychological and spiritual truth that good habits are powerful aids in your efforts. Good habits are automatic psychological machines that enable you to perform specific acctions without much effort of will; they help to make the performance of difficult actions easier. It is by _regularity_ and _continuity_ that you create good habits of meditation and other spiritual practices. 

The minutes are more important than the years. If you do not fill in the minutes of your life with thoughts of God, the years will slip by and when you need Him most you will not be able to feel His presence. But if you do fill in the minutes of you life with the thought of God, you will find that the years of your life automatically will be saturated with the consciousness of God. Never think of tomorrow. Take care of today and all the tomorrows will take care of themselves. Do not wait until tomorrow to meditate. Do not wait until tomorrow to be good. Be good now! Be calm now! It will be the turning point of your life.

## Story: A Saint Who Chose a King as His Spiritual Guide


> [!NOTE] Intro
> _The following is from India's ancient spiritual lore. It is one of many such stories frequently told by [[Paramhansa Yogananda]] to illustrate basic principles of dharma, the eternal laws of right actions._

Long ago there lived a great sage named Vyasa. He was the writer of the Hindu scripture, the [[Bhagavad Gita]]. By his divine power he invoked a saintly soul to enter the womb of his wife. As the baby grew within the womb, he taught the unborn child the secrets of the scriptures through the subconscious mind of the mother. This baby, when born, was named [[Shukadeva]]. Because of his prenatal training, he proved to be a unique child. At the age of seven he already was versed in the difficult Hindu scriptures and was ready to renounce the world and devote himself fully to his spiritual quest.

In India it is customary for a devotee to seek out spiritual teachers until a true mast is found,[^17] one who is recognised as the devotee's own God-chosen guru.

Spiritual novices, through fitful inner urgings, receive instruction from various sources; but when one's spiritual ardour becomes very great, God sends a guru. God used the divine soul-vehicle of the guru as His messenger, or instrument, to bring the aspirant back to the soul's true spiritual home in Omnipresence.

The unusual child, [[Shukadeva]], had a tremendous urge to seek the Divine. In his fervent search he was directed by his father to go to King Janaka, the ruler of the state of Videha. As Shukadeva entered the palace, he saw the king sitting on an elaborate jewel-encrusted golden throne. The king was surrounded by courtiers, and by court damsels who were fanning him with palm leaves. This sight shocked Shukadeva. He turned back and started to walk briskly out of the palace, muttering to himself: "A matter-soaked king! How could a worldly man be my teacher?"

By King Janaka was both king and saint. He was in the world, but not of it. Highly advanced spiritually, he telepathically sensed the thoughts of the fleeing Shukadeva, and sent a messenger after the body, commanding him to come back. Thus master and devotee met. The king sent his attendants away, and at once entered an absorbing discourse on God. Four hours passed; Shukadeva was getting restless and hungry, but he dared not disturb God-intoxicated King Janaka. 

Another hour passed. Suddenly, two breathless messengers arrived, exclaiming: "Your Majesty, the whole city is on fire! The flames threaten to spread to the palace. Won't you come and supervise the efforts to fight it?"

The king replied: "I am too busy discussing the all-protecting God with my friend, Shukadeva. I have no time for anything else. Go and help the others put out the fire."

When another hour passed, the same two messengers came running to King Janaka and cried: "Your Majesty, please flee! The flames have caught the palace and are fast approaching your chamber."

The king answered: "Never mind! Don't disturb me! I am drinking the nectar of God with my friend. Go! Do the best you can."

Shukadeva was puzzled by the king's attitude, but he too tried to remain unaffected by the excitement. A short time later two scorched messengers leaped in front of the king, shouting: "Mighty King! Behold the flames approaching your throne! Run before both of you are burned!"

The king replied: "Save yourselves; I do not fear the audacity of destructive flames." His messengers fled. The flames leaped toward the pile of books that Shukadeva had by his side; but the king sat motionless, indifferent.

The alarmed Shukadeva lost his poise; he half rose form his seat and began slapping at the flames in order to save his precious books. King Janaka then smilingly waved his hand; the fire miraculously disappeared. Shukadeva, in great awe sank back to his seat.

The king said calmly: "O young Shukadeva, you thought of me as a pleasure and matter-drenched king, but look at yourself! You forsook the all-protecting thought of God to save a pile of books, while I paid no attention to my burning kingdom and palace. God worked this miracle to show you that although you are a renunciant, you are more attached to your books than to God, or than I am to my kingdom, even though I live in the world instead of a hermitage. Attachment to possessions, and possession itself, is the source of misery. In this world we do not own anything; we are only given the use of things. Some have more to use that others; but remember, the millionaire and the poor man alike have to leave everything, all possessions, when death comes." Humbled, the young Shukadeva then recognised the saint-king to be his guru.

King Janaka put Shukadeva through a process of discipline to teach him the art of living in the world without acquiring misery-making attachment to it. One day the gave his new disciple two cup-shaped lamps, filled to the brim with oil. Janaka said: "Hold a lamp on the palm of each had, and walk through all the rooms of the palace. Come back to me after have seen everything; but remember, I will refuse to train you further if you spill a single drop form the lamps."

King Janaka instructed messengers to accompany Shukadeva and to refill the lamps with oil as quickly as they burned down. It was a hard test; but after some hours Shukadeva returned triumphantly without having spilled any oil. 

The king said: "Young Shukadeva, tell me in detail what you saw in each chamber."

To this Shukadeva replied: "Royal Preceptor, my only accomplishment was that I did not spill any oil on your carpets. My mind was so concentrated on the thought of not dripping oil that I did not notice anything in the rooms."

King Janaka then declared: "I am disappointed! You have not completely passed my test. My injunctions were that you should see everything in all the chambers of the palace and that you should not drip any oil from the lamps. God back with the lamps, _and_ remember, do not spill the oil while you look carefully at everything in the palace."

After several more hours, Shukadeva calmly returned. He had not allowed any oil to drip, nor was he perspiring with excitement as before. He could answer all the king's questions about the contents, however minute, of all the palace chambers.

King Janaka was pleased. "My son," he said gently, "one should not live a one-sided life, thinking only of God and neglecting one's duties in the world, such as your concentrating on the oil lamps and not seeing the palace. On the second trip you kept your attention principally on the lamps, seeing to it that you did not spill any oil, but at the same time you thoroughly saw everything. So should you keep your attention on God, not letting a drop slip away from the lamp of God-revealing wisdom, and yet devote part of your attention to performing efficiently the God-given duties of maintaining yourself and others given into your charge."

---
The two morals of this story illustrate the basis of the world's troubles: one-sidedness and attachment. We were intended to live in the world, but not to be bound by it; to fulfil our material duties in life, but to centre our consciousness in neglect of outward duties, most people today have spilled all the precious oil of inner God-perception while racing after external experiences. And unlike the wise King Janaka, who was detached from the vast material treasures of his kingdom, modern men and women dote on their transitory possessions as the sole sources of happiness. 

The mass effect of attachment to material things and indifference to spiritual development has let the world to selfishness, poverty amidst plenty, and finally to widespread economic depression. In the individual, forgetting God and clinging to possessions brings sorrow and dissatisfaction. Hence, those who want the unlimited Divine Power to work for them in business and family affairs and to bestow on them lasting peace and happiness should be as earnest in seeking God as they are in pursuing the materialities He has created; as devoted to meditation as they are to earning money. All who endeavour to find God first, and to hold that divine "oil" every steady in their consciousness, will gain both enduring inner happiness and the outer success of acquiring their material needs. 

We must not feel we are too busy to try to contact God. Should God stop our hearts, we would be unable to keep any of our business or social engagements. Since all our success depends upon powers borrowed from God, truly that person is successful in life who devotes time to God-communion as well as to material duties. And one who enjoys the good and beautiful things of this world, yet refuses to be possessed by them, is one who finds lasting happiness. 

So remember, dear student, that to be mentally and physically idle, neglecting to perform the duties required of you, is to be forsaken by God and man. And to carry our your duties but forget God is to be like the mule carrying a bag of gold: It gets a little satisfaction of receiving food as a reward for carrying its load, but it does not use or appreciate the priceless treasure on its back. Similarly, one can receive a little satisfaction through performing one's duties, but miss the greatest happiness in life, which comes from being conscious of the God-Bliss you are carrying within your soul. To perform all proper outer duties with concentration, while being inwardly aware of the gold of God's presence, is the ideal mode of living - the way of everlasting happiness.

## Supplement: Training the Eyes for Meditation

The human eyes change their position and angle of vision according to our changing thoughts. There is a correspondence between the position of the eyes (and hence the direction of the gaze) and the mental state.

When one is in the conscious wakeful state, the eyes and gaze are straight ahead - focused on the outer world of matter. When one is asleep (the subconscious state), they go down; and when one is in the superconscious state, the eyes and gaze naturally turn upward.

In wakefulness the eyelids are constantly winking and the eyeballs are continually in motion. Restless movement of the eyes reflect the restlessness of the thoughts of the mind. 

By closing the eyes and lowering their gaze, one can invoke sleep; and by keeping the eyes upward and perfectly still - concentrating on the point between the eyebrows - one can induce the superconscious state.

Hence, one of the first steps in mastering meditation is learning to steady the eyes so that they do not restlessly shift to and fro. The yogis teach that the best way to develop this ability is to practise looking straight ahead with the eyes focused on a definite poihnt, such as the head of a pin that is fixed to the wall. Keep the eeylids and eyeballs steady, without quivering. Never mind if the eyes water. Do not strain. Practise this exercise of training the eys during leisure periods for a few minutes every now and then. 

When you have learned to gaze straight ahead without blinking or your eyes watering, life your eyes )keeping the chin parallel to the ground, not turning the head upward) and concentrate on any definite point on the ceiling (or on the wall near the ceiling) of the room you are meditating in. 

When you are accustomed to doing this, quickly and gently transfer the gaze, without straining the eyes, to concentrate on the midpoint between the eyebrows, with half-open eyes. Learn to practise this little until you can fix your gaze as though looking out through a point between the eyebrows for a long time without blinking, strain, or watering of the eyes. This will greatly aid you in correct practice of the techniques of meditation taught in these _Lessons_.

The beginner at meditation may feel slight eyestrain during initial efforst to concentrate the visoin with half-closed eyes at the oint between the eyebrows, owing to the unfamiliar practice. This will vanish as one becomes used to concentrating upward on the spiritual eyes. keeping the eyes neither fully closed nor fully opened enables the meditator to avoid both the subconscious state of slumber and the restless, outward-directed state of conscious wakefulness. 

The important point for meditation is this: The eys should be upturned in a steady gaze toward the _Kutastha_ (Christ) centre, and should not move. If you can do so without restless fluttering of the eyelids or undue strain, you may practise with eyelids half closed. Alternatively, the eyelids may be completely closed if this is more comfortable or if it makes it easier to keep the eyes unblinking and motionless. HOwever, when the eyelids are completely closed, the eyes have a tendency to relax from the upturned gaze, sometimes turning downward and producing sleep.

Should you be bothered with hallucinations, which generally spring from the subconscious mind and are false inpersonations of spiritual visions, keep the eyes open, turned upward, and fixed on the point between the eyebrows. Meditating with eyes open or half open and gently (without strain) concentrated between the eyebrows will prevent hallucinations. 

Once the meditator has succeeded in keeping the eyes fixed at the [[Kutastha]] centre, it does not matter whether the eyelids are half closed or completely closed. In deep meditation the eyelids have a tendency to open slightly, automatically. In this state the upper and lower eyelids relax, producing the half-open, half-closed position - through the gaze remains upward toward the point between the eyebrows. 

Perseverance in correct practice, as well as the deepening of concentration and interiorization that gradually comes in meditation, will enable the meditator to attain the correct position of the eyes - motionless, relaxed, yet fixed at the Christ-[[Kutastha]] centre. 

Yogis who have mastered the ability to enter [[Samadhi]] at will can completely absorb their consciousness is the [[Kutastha]] centre with the eyes open, half closed, or fully closed.
