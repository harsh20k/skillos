Heilmeier's Catechism is a set of eight fundamental questions developed by George H. Heilmeier, former director of the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), to evaluate proposed research programs and guide the creation of innovative, high-impact projects[1][2][4][7]. 

### Purpose and Origin
- The catechism was intended to help managers, researchers, and reviewers identify the value, novelty, risks, and requirements of proposed efforts, especially in high-risk, high-reward environments like DARPA[1][2][4].

### The Eight Questions
These are the classic questions of Heilmeier's Catechism:
- What are you trying to do? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.
- How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?
- What is new in your approach, and why do you think it will be successful?
- Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?
- What are the risks?
- How much will it cost?
- How long will it take?
- What are the midterm and final “exams” to check for success?[1][2][4][5][7]

### Application
- Used primarily for evaluating research project proposals, the Catechism is a template for ensuring projects are clearly defined, feasible, and impactful before receiving funding[2][7].
- It is widely used in research organizations and in grant proposal processes to help clarify project goals, methods, and significance[7].

### Impact and Legacy
- The Heilmeier Catechism is praised for its simplicity, directness, and ability to provoke critical discussion and iteration in research planning[1][2][4].
- It remains an influential tool in science and engineering communities for proposal evaluation and project development[1][7].
