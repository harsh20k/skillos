# Becoming Who You Want to Be

Almost 20% of 2025 is almost over. A few days back I was asking Suman about her this years' resolutions and an interesting conversation took place. 

She told me some objective that she thought was important and told me that it was her this year's resolution and that's what she was trying to do. But when she asked me the same question I could not answer her because I had not really thought about it and could not give a specific answer.

I mean its like all of us; we have so many things that we wish to do this year and many things that we wish to better. But how many of us do actually take time to actually think about those things and evaluate them somehow on some parameters so we can come to a conclusion of selecting some attainable new year resolutions? Not many I suppose.

The part of the conversation that I found specially interesting was the following question I asked her and her sad answer that was filled with truth. I asked her plainly "Is that your actual resolution? Meaning that you have actually given time to think about it and have a specific laid out plan to achieve it?"

Her answer was a simple "No" and I think that's the sad truth of New year's resolutions. 

That's what gives bad reputation to the "New Year resolutions". We often just try to think of something on the spot that we want to have this year without actually thinking about it. And the results are often the same- forgotten resolutions, same daily lives and same ongoing habits and routines.

And should it be surprising when near the end of the year we feel that nothing has actually changed? I don't think so. 

# Identity Over Resolutions: Becoming the Change you Seek

This makes me think of how we can incorporate resolutions as a part of identity and habits and routines just like mentioned by James Clear in "Atomic Habits". 

Instead of thinking what to do this year, what I feel would be more helpful would be an identity shift. For example, the wish of writing more or writing daily might get you somewhere but a simple change in identity( I am a writer ) might take you much more closer to the end goal. 

![[Pasted image 20250315102955.jpg]]

So taking this into account I have made certain core identity shifts that I wish would be beneficial for my growth. 

1. I am a Coder/ Developer.
2. I am an Artist.
3. I am a writer.
4. I am a meditator. 

---
Now for bringing these identities to life in my current state all I need to do is to maintain some form of daily practice or streaks. 

As a coder I have to write code, read code think about code almost everyday not for 5 hours but only a minute is also enough. And as an artist I cannot let days go by without drawing. Just like a writer just writes, I just have to write everyday not create a masterpiece. As a practitioner of meditation a single day without meditation should be shameful for me.

---
Now the conversation with the self changes from "I have to do this everyday" to "I am a football player so I play everyday". Instantly almost all of the friction, at least in principle vanishes. 

I really liked what Jordan Perterson said in one of his lectures "We are our own worst employees and our own worst bosses".

And he explained that we often whip ourselves to death to do something torturing ourselves with guilt of not accomplishing it or comparing with others. That will be obviously counter productive.

Further, we often just complete ignore the idea of doing anything productive with our time. Not because we have something else to do but "I just don't want to / I will do it later".

Our boss and employee are fighting with each other all the time and forget the fact that both of them are on the same team!

Have a great year! (at least rest 80% of it.. )

# In short...

1. Most New Year’s resolutions fail due to lack of planning and commitment.
2. Instead of setting vague goals, adopt **identity-based habits** (e.g., “I am a writer” instead of “I will write more”).
3. Daily practice, even for a minute, reinforces these identities and builds consistency.
4. Shifting from **“I have to”** to **“I am”** removes friction and makes habits feel natural.
5. Jordan Peterson’s idea: We are often our **own worst bosses and employees**, either pushing too hard or procrastinating.
6. The key to growth? Work with yourself, not against yourself.
7. Make the remaining 80% of 2025 count! 🚀
