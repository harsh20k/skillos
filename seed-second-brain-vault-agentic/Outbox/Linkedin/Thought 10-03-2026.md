Sometimes the most interesting lessons come from a 5‑minute whiteboard exercise.

Yesterday in our serverless lecture, we had to sketch an architecture for a car dealership (or any similar business) and then present it. No time for polish. No time for "perfect". Just: here’s the problem, what would you actually build?

As I was scrambling to put boxes and arrows together, something became very clear to me.

Cost and security can’t be an afterthought in architecture.

If you start with "features" and only later ask, "Oh, by the way, how much will this cost?" or "Is this even secure?", you’re already in trouble. The trade‑offs are baked in from the first decision – which services you pick, how data flows, what you log, who can touch what.

There is no such thing as the perfect architecture. There is only an architecture that fits a specific set of requirements, constraints, and expectations – including budget and risk.

So now I’m trying to practice more of these small, time‑boxed architecture challenges. Not to chase perfection, but to train the habit of asking, right from the first sketch:

What are we optimizing for – and what are we willing to pay and risk for it?
