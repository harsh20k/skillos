Today we had an industry showcase from Deepsense at university. Program managers Alex and Abby came in and shared what they actually work on and what trips up new students the most.

About half their projects are based on computer vision. The rest? Forecasting models, edge deployment, and a mix of other applied ML. But the part that stuck with me wasn't the tech stack. It was the gaps they see in people coming in.

Unsupervised learning. Unstructured data. Model evaluation and selection, actually comparing models instead of just picking one and hoping. These are where new students struggle. And honestly? I've felt that too. It's easy to train a model. It's harder to know if it's the right one.

Their advice was refreshingly practical. Explain your projects clearly on your resume. Don't wait for perfect datasets, find ugly ones and work with them. And maybe the most important one: add projects even when they fail. You still learn a lot. The model might not work. The pipeline might break. But that's where the real learning happens.

I left thinking: we spend so much time chasing the next shiny thing. Maybe the better move is to ship something messy, document what went wrong, and show that we can think through tradeoffs.
