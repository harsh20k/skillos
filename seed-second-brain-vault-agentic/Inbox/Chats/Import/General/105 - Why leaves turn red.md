# Why leaves turn red

**Priority:** Medium

## Conversation

### Assistant

Great observation 🌿 — you’re right: in most parts of India, we don’t really see that classic **red autumn foliage** the way we do in Canada, the US, or Europe.  

Here’s why:  

---

### 1. **Climate Differences**  
- In temperate regions (like North America or Europe), fall brings **short daylight, cool nights, and sunny days**. This combination triggers anthocyanin (red pigment) production.  
- In most of India, especially the plains and tropics, the climate doesn’t create those sharp **day–night temperature differences** in autumn. Nights aren’t cold enough to trap sugars in leaves, so anthocyanins aren’t produced in large amounts.  

---

### 2. **Tree Species**  
- Indian forests are dominated by **evergreens, tropical deciduous trees, and monsoon-adapted species**. These trees usually have carotenoids (yellow/orange) but **not much anthocyanin** in their leaves.  
- Maples, oaks, sweetgums — the famous “red” species of North America/Europe — are **not native** to most of India.  

---

### 3. **Leaf-Shedding Pattern**  
- In temperate regions: leaves fall because of winter cold → dramatic color change first.  
- In much of India: leaves fall due to **dry season stress** (heat and drought) rather than winter cold. Trees often shed leaves directly, turning brown or yellow, not red.  

---

### 4. **Where in India you *might* see red**  
- In the **Himalayas**, where conditions are cooler and more temperate, you can find some maples, cherries, and other species that do turn red or purple in autumn.  
- But in the plains and tropics, it’s rare.  

---

**Leaves in India don’t usually turn red because the climate doesn’t trigger anthocyanin production, and most native tree species don’t rely on that pigment.**
