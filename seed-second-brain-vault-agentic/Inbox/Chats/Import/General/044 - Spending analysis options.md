Here is a clear, practical way to interpret your number:

## **1. Your number: 11 no-transaction days out of 81 (13.6%)**  
This means **you are transacting on 86.4% of days** — almost every day.

This is **normal for students, newcomers, or anyone settling into a new country**. Your pattern is not “bad,” but it does show **high frequency spending**, even when amounts are small.

## **2. What is ideal?**

There is no universal standard, but **financial planners** use a simple heuristic:

### **Ideal: 25–40% of days should have no transactions**  
This means:
- 2–3 days per week without spending  
- OR 8–12 no-spend days per month  
- OR ~30–40% no-spend rate  

Why?  
Because a higher “no-transaction day ratio” normally means:
- You’re batching expenses  
- You're minimizing impulse buys  
- Less leakage in food/delivery/transport  
- Better control over discretionary spending  

### How your number compares:
- **You: 13.6%**
- **Ideal: 25–40%**

So you are **below ideal**, spending on far more days than a typical optimized household budget.

But again — **this is expected for you** because:
- You just moved  
- Setting up a new home  
- Lots of essentials + groceries  
- No car → small and frequent transport/spending  
- Eating out is part of student life and convenience  

## **3. What would “healthy” look like for you specifically?**

Given your situation (student + new immigrant + shared expenses), a realistic target is:

### **Phase 1 (Now–3 months): Target 20–25% no-spend days**
That’s about:
- **6–8 no-spend days per month**
- **1–2 per week**

### **Phase 2 (After 3 months): Target 30–35%**
- **9–11 no-spend days per month**

This is achievable by:
- Buying groceries in batches  
- Avoiding 2–3 very small daily expenses (Tims, small grocery top-ups)
- Simplifying subscriptions  
- Minimizing Uber/eating out clustering  

## **4. Is your 13.6% concerning?**

Not concerning.  
It just reflects a transitional phase.

But if you're planning:

Then gradually raising the no-transaction days % is a **very strong predictor of financial discipline**.
