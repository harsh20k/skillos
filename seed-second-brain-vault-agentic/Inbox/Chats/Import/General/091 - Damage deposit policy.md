# Damage deposit policy

**Priority:** Medium

## Conversation

### Assistant

Thanks for explaining — that’s a stressful situation, and you're **legally protected** in several ways under the **Nova Scotia Residential Tenancies Act**, especially if:

- You’re on a **fixed-term lease** (e.g., 1-year lease)
- You’re trying to end it **early**
- The landlord is **threatening to withhold your deposit or take legal action** unless you “find a replacement tenant”

Let’s break this down clearly:

---

## 🧾 1. Can your landlord force you to find a replacement tenant?

**No — legally, it is the landlord’s responsibility to re-rent the unit**, not yours.

Under Nova Scotia tenancy law:
> “The landlord must make reasonable efforts to re-rent the unit if the tenant ends the lease early.”  
> *(Source: Nova Scotia Tenancies Program Guide)*

You may *help* by suggesting a replacement (called "assignment" or "subletting" depending on terms), but:
- It is **not legally required** that you find the new tenant
- The landlord **cannot threaten legal action** just because you didn’t find one
- They **must attempt to mitigate losses** (i.e., rent to someone else)

---

## 🔓 2. When can you legally end the lease?

### a. If you’re on **month-to-month**:  
You can end the lease with **1 full month's written notice** before the next rent due date.

### b. If you’re on a **fixed-term lease** (e.g., until next year):  
You’re expected to stay until the end unless:
- You **mutually agree** to terminate early  
- There are **valid reasons** (health, safety, family violence, etc.)  
- OR, you can request **assignment** (new person takes over lease)  
  - The landlord **cannot unreasonably refuse** a qualified replacement

---

## 💰 3. Can the landlord withhold your **damage deposit**?

Only **under specific, legal conditions**:
- **Unpaid rent**
- **Documented damage** (beyond normal wear and tear)
- Only if they file **Form R** within **10 days** of the end of tenancy

➡️ They **cannot** withhold it just because you moved out or didn’t find a new tenant.  
➡️ And they **must return it within 10 days**, or **you can file Form S** to get it back.

---

## ⚖️ 4. Can she “take legal action” against you?

Anyone can threaten legal action — but here’s what’s **realistic**:
- If you abandon a fixed-term lease without agreement, the landlord can *try* to claim **lost rent** (e.g., if the unit stays vacant).
- But she **must show she made reasonable effort** to re-rent it.
- And even then, she would **file with the Residential Tenancies Program**, not “take you to court” immediately.

In practice: these threats are often used to pressure tenants — but **you have legal recourse**, and **a written trail helps you**.

---

## ✅ What You Should Do Now

1. **Write a polite email** stating:
   - You are willing to cooperate
   - You are not legally required to find a replacement
   - You understand your rights under the Residential Tenancies Act
   - Request clarity in writing, and mention that threats of withholding deposit are improper

2. **Ask for mutual lease termination**, or formally **request assignment** (you suggest a replacement; landlord screens)

3. **Document everything in writing** — no calls or WhatsApp only. This helps if a dispute goes to Residential Tenancies.
