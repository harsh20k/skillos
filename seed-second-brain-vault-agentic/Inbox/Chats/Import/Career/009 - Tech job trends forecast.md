# Tech Industry Trends in Canada (2025-2027) – National and Atlantic Outlook

## Canada’s Tech Industry: Growth and Key Trends (2023-2025)  
**Steady Expansion:** Canada’s tech sector has been on a steady growth trajectory. Net tech employment reached about **1.45 million workers in 2024**, up ~1.9% from the previous year【1†L109-L117】. This continued rise follows a brief pandemic-era dip in 2020, adding roughly **290,500 tech jobs since 2019**【1†L111-L119】. By 2025, net tech employment is forecast to grow another 1.4% to **~1.46 million** workers【15†L115-L123】, accounting for approximately **6.8% of Canada’s total workforce**【15†L115-L123】. In economic terms, the tech industry contributes about **$131.6 billion CAD** (≈5.8% of GDP) and comprises over **83,000 businesses** nationwide【15†L145-L152】【19†L127-L133】. This means tech is a significant and growing pillar of the Canadian economy.

**Major Tech Hubs:** Growth is concentrated in traditional tech hubs like **Ontario (especially the Toronto area)**, Québec (Montreal), British Columbia (Vancouver), and Alberta (Calgary) – all of which have above-average concentrations of tech workers【15†L139-L147】【19†L117-L125】. For instance, the Toronto metro alone hosts **414,667 tech workers (10.7% of its workforce)**【19†L118-L125】, making it the largest tech talent pool in Canada. However, smaller regions are rising: cities outside the “big three” have seen tech job postings remain **~26% above pre-pandemic levels**, even as postings in Toronto/Montreal/Vancouver dipped ~15% below pre-pandemic levels【14†L129-L137】. This suggests that **emerging tech markets** – including mid-sized cities – are playing a bigger role in Canada’s tech landscape, aided by remote work and cost-of-living advantages. Employers are increasingly open to recruiting in **secondary markets** to access talent with less competition【19†L121-L129】.

**High-Demand Skills and Roles:** Across Canada, **demand for specialized tech skills remains robust**. While overall tech hiring has become a bit more selective post-pandemic (focusing on high-impact roles over generalist entry-level positions)【14†L53-L61】【14†L65-L73】, companies continue to seek expertise in areas like software development, data analytics, cloud and AI, and cybersecurity. In fact, **job growth is anticipated across both foundational IT roles (tech support, network/infrastructure, software development) and rapidly emerging fields (artificial intelligence, advanced data analytics)**【15†L121-L129】. Over the next decade, tech occupations are projected to grow **1.77× faster than overall employment** in Canada【19†L77-L85】. Roles such as *software engineers and designers, information systems managers, cybersecurity specialists, data scientists, data analysts/administrators,* and *business systems specialists* are expected to see some of the **strongest growth (potentially more than doubling)**【15†L129-L137】【19†L81-L89】.  For example, the number of software engineers could grow by **+163%** and cybersecurity specialists by **+131%** (relative to overall job market growth) in the coming years【19†L81-L89】. This trend reflects how tech skills are now essential in every industry – **“digitally fluent” employees are in demand in organizations of all sizes and sectors**【15†L121-L129】.

**Rising Wages and Talent Competition:** The continued growth in tech jobs has put upward pressure on salaries. The **median tech worker wage in Canada is about $97,200 CAD**, roughly **48% higher** than the national median for all jobs【15†L145-L152】【19†L127-L135】. Employers face fierce competition to attract and retain skilled workers, especially in hot fields. Even with recent high-profile tech layoffs globally, in Canada many companies report talent shortages in specialized roles. Notably, immigration has historically helped supply tech talent (immigrants comprise *35% of programmers and 43% of engineers* in Canada)【14†L100-L108】. Going forward, any shifts in immigration policy or slower inflows of international tech talent could tighten the labor market further【14†L100-L108】. Overall, to hire for in-demand roles by 2027, companies are offering not just high pay but also career development, flexibility, and meaningful work to entice candidates【19†L127-L135】. For job seekers, this means the financial upside in tech is strong, but they should also build specialized skills (e.g. AI, cloud, cybersecurity) to stand out as hiring becomes more selective.

## Atlantic Canada Tech Industry Trends  
The Atlantic region (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Newfoundland & Labrador, and Prince Edward Island) represents a **small but growing** share of Canada’s tech ecosystem. Collectively, these four provinces account for roughly **60,000 tech workers** (just ~4% of Canada’s tech workforce) as of the mid-2020s【4†L419-L427】. Despite the smaller size, **Atlantic Canada’s tech sector has been expanding faster than many other local industries**, thanks to a combination of regional initiatives, a surge in skilled immigrants, and the spread of remote-friendly tech jobs.

- **Strong Growth in Nova Scotia:** Nova Scotia is the Atlantic leader in tech employment. In 2023, it had roughly **28,000 tech workers**【4†L419-L427】, nearly half the Atlantic region’s tech workforce. The province has enjoyed robust growth – tech sector employment in NS grew about **3.6% annually on average (forecast for 2024–2026)【10†L266-L274】**, outpacing the overall provincial job growth (~2.1%). Halifax, Nova Scotia’s capital, is emerging as a genuine tech hub: over the last five years its tech talent pool expanded **64.2%**【13†L75-L83】 – one of the fastest growth rates in North America. (Halifax was recently ranked 5th among **“up-and-coming” tech markets** in a 2024 CBRE report【13†L75-L83】.) This growth has been fueled by a cluster of startups and innovation hubs (e.g. in fintech and ocean technology), investment in research (health records digitization, ocean tech, etc.), and an influx of tech graduates and newcomers. Nova Scotia now hosts about **38,400 jobs in professional, scientific and technical services** (NAICS 54)【8†L133-L139】, and about a third of these are in **computer systems design/IT services**【8†L102-L110】. Recent federal funding has targeted local tech niches – for example, **$5.5 million invested in cybersecurity initiatives** in Nova Scotia, and support for Halifax’s growing fintech community【10†L289-L297】. With this momentum, Nova Scotia is expected to continue leading Atlantic Canada in tech job creation through 2026, adding well over **1,300 tech jobs in 2024 alone**【15†L133-L139】. By early 2027, Nova Scotia’s tech employment will likely have grown further (potentially approaching 30,000 tech workers), with high demand in areas like software development, IT project management, and analytics.

- **New Brunswick’s Niche Focus:** New Brunswick is the second-largest Atlantic province in tech, with about **18,700 tech workers** as of 2023【4†L423-L430】. The province is known for its **cybersecurity cluster** – it hosts large cybersecurity R&D employers and the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity at UNB, making cyber talent a local specialty【8†L122-L130】. NB’s tech sector growth has been more modest, however. It’s projected around **1.4% annual growth (2024–26)**【10†L266-L274】, roughly on par with NB’s overall job growth. In fact, recent data showed a slight net dip in tech jobs in NB (a small decline of ~372 jobs in a year)【4†L423-L431】【4†L433-L441】, possibly due to workforce turnover or consolidation. Nonetheless, New Brunswick’s **high-tech services** (like IT consulting and computer design) remain a critical part of its economy – about one-third of Atlantic Canada’s professional/tech services workforce is in NB【8†L122-L130】. The **job vacancy rate** in NB’s tech sector is around **3.4% (Q1 2024)【10†L273-L281】**, indicating continued openings for skilled workers. Key employers (including global firms running delivery centers and local startups) are still hiring, especially for cybersecurity specialists, software developers, and IT support roles. By 2027, NB is expected to have steady if not spectacular tech job growth – likely a few hundred new roles added each year – with ongoing demand for security experts and IT professionals to support its firms.

- **Newfoundland & Labrador and PEI:** The remaining Atlantic provinces have **smaller tech ecosystems** but with distinct niches. Newfoundland and Labrador had around **9,200 tech workers** in 2023【4†L427-L432】, and its tech growth has been flat or slightly negative recently (NL saw a minor net loss of ~550 tech jobs in one year)【4†L425-L433】【4†L435-L443】. However, NL boasts a notable **financial-tech (fintech) and software sector** in St. John’s – exemplified by companies like Verafin (financial crime software) – as well as growing ocean technology and energy-tech projects. Prince Edward Island is the smallest province (only ~3,900 tech workers【4†L427-L432】), but it has invested in **bioscience and IT**; PEI’s professional/technical services sector is actually projected to grow fastest in the region (about **4.2% annually** from 2024–26)【8†L83-L87】【10†L266-L274】, albeit from a very small base. We can expect these two provinces to see gradual tech job increases by 2027 as digital adoption spreads in all sectors (for instance, PEI’s bioscience firms and NL’s offshore energy sector increasingly need data analysts, engineers, and IT support). **Combined, NL and PEI might add only a few hundred tech jobs per year**, but any growth is valuable for diversifying their local economies.

**Atlantic Outlook – Opportunities and Challenges:** Overall, Atlantic Canada’s tech sector is **set to expand through 2026 and into 2027**, even if at a moderate pace. Projections show Atlantic **tech-related industries growing ~2.7% annually** (2024-2026) on average, faster than the region’s overall employment growth【8†L79-L87】【10†L262-L270】. By 2027, the region should have a larger tech workforce than ever before. Notably, **Nova Scotia and PEI are leading this growth** (each forecast well above 3%/yr)【10†L266-L274】, while NB and NL grow more slowly【10†L266-L274】. This means **thousands of job openings** will be available. In Nova Scotia alone, about **6,155 job opportunities** in engineering, science and IT occupations are expected **through 2026**【6†L77-L85】 – a rate of openings (~4.9%/year of the workforce) that is the highest among occupational groups. Many of these openings come from industry growth (especially in IT, projected **+3.2% yearly** in NS)【6†L77-L85】, as well as retirements (attrition). Importantly, **tech talent shortages** remain a concern in Atlantic Canada. For example, Nova Scotia’s tech vacancy rate (4.4%) is higher than the overall job vacancy rate, indicating companies are competing for a limited pool of skilled workers【10†L273-L281】. Employers in the region are keen to attract newcomers and graduates to fill roles, and initiatives like the Atlantic Immigration Program are designed to bring in tech professionals. In summary, Atlantic Canada offers a growing but **less saturated job market** for tech workers – for instance, Halifax and Fredericton may have **less intense competition for talent compared to Toronto**【19†L121-L129】, making these cities attractive for new graduates or companies looking to expand. The region’s tech scene in 2027 will likely feature a **continuation of current trends**: steady growth in software development, IT services, and niche fields (cybersecurity, fintech, ocean tech, biosciences), supported by ongoing digital transformation efforts in both the private and public sectors【10†L281-L289】.

## Tech Job Landscape by Early 2027 – What to Expect  
By the beginning of 2027, the Canadian tech job landscape is expected to be **expansive and dynamic**, shaped by the trends outlined above. Nationally, if current growth rates hold, Canada’s tech workforce could be approaching **1.5–1.6 million workers** (roughly estimating from the 2024 baseline) – meaning tens of thousands of new tech jobs will have been created in the mid-2020s【15†L115-L123】. This growth will likely **outpace overall job market growth**, as digital roles infiltrate every industry from finance to natural resources【19†L77-L85】. For example, banks and governments in Canada are investing heavily in AI, cybersecurity, and cloud services, creating demand for specialists in those areas. **Artificial intelligence and automation** will be much more ingrained in business operations by 2027, so skills in machine learning and data science should be even more sought-after (AI-related job postings have already more than doubled since 2020)【14†L75-L83】. Similarly, **cybersecurity** is expected to remain a top priority – high-profile cyber threats have driven a $5 million+ investment in Atlantic Canada’s cyber workforce recently【10†L289-L297】, and by 2027 most organizations will have expanded their security teams. We can also anticipate that **cloud computing and software engineering** roles stay in high demand as companies continue migrating systems online and developing new digital products. In fact, over the next few years, **software developers/engineers are consistently listed among the most in-demand roles** across Canada【6†L97-L105】【8†L169-L177】. 

In Atlantic Canada specifically, **early 2027 will likely see a healthier tech job market than ever before**. Nova Scotia’s tech hub in Halifax is poised to grow – recent rankings already recognize Halifax as a burgeoning tech talent market【13†L75-L83】. The region will keep benefitting from the **“spread-out” tech workforce** trend, where more tech professionals choose smaller cities for affordability and lifestyle, working either for local startups or remotely for national firms【14†L129-L137】. We expect Atlantic tech employment to reach new highs (potentially on track for ~85,000+ jobs in the broad professional/tech sector by 2026【10†L253-L262】, with roughly 25,000–30,000 core tech jobs in NS alone). Given the projected growth rates, **Nova Scotia might be adding around 1,000+ tech jobs each year** up to 2027, and PEI a smaller but significant amount proportionally【10†L266-L274】. New tech graduates are also steadily entering the workforce; for example, Halifax saw ~1,069 tech degree graduates in 2022【13†L89-L97】, and these numbers are likely rising with new educational programs. By 2027, Atlantic Canada’s tech scene should feature more mid-sized companies and startups (especially in Halifax and Fredericton) and possibly satellite offices of larger firms attracted by lower costs. One can also predict that certain **tech sub-sectors will flourish**: e.g. **clean tech and green IT projects** (supported by government sustainable growth plans), continued expansion of **fintech** (Halifax launched a new fintech hub in 2025), and growth in **interactive media and gaming** (as suggested by local gaming studios news) – all contributing to new job creation.

**Job Market Characteristics:** A few qualitative aspects likely to define the 2027 tech job landscape: 

- **Competitive Hiring for Specialists:** Employers will still be competing hard for people with **specialized skills**, while entry-level/generalist tech candidates may face more competition from each other【14†L65-L73】. The “post-pandemic hiring frenzy” has normalized, but unemployment in tech remains low. Nearly **57% of tech job postings in 2024 specified experience requirements【1†L135-L143】**, which indicates many roles are not entry-level. By 2027, we expect a continued preference for candidates who can demonstrate hands-on experience or niche expertise (e.g. cloud architecture, machine learning engineering, full-stack development).

- **Geographic Flexibility:** Remote and hybrid work will likely remain prevalent in tech. This benefits Atlantic Canada, as skilled workers can live in cities like Halifax or Moncton and work for companies anywhere. It also means local firms must compete not just regionally but nationally for talent. However, Atlantic Canada’s quality of life and lower cost can be a selling point to attract tech professionals from bigger cities.

- **Talent Shortage vs. Upskilling:** If immigration policies slow international influx slightly【14†L100-L108】, Canadian employers by 2027 will be even more focused on **upskilling domestic talent** and tapping into new grad pipelines. We might see more partnerships with universities (e.g. Dalhousie, UNB) and **training programs** in Atlantic Canada to produce the skilled workers needed. On the flip side, as a job seeker, having advanced credentials (like a Master’s degree) will give you an edge, as a significant **22.6% of workers in tech/professional services in Atlantic Canada now hold a degree above bachelor’s【8†L146-L154】**, reflecting a premium on education in this sector.

## Career Outlook for a New Master’s Graduate (Your Profile)  
For someone **graduating around 2026 with a Master’s in Applied Computer Science (MACS)** from Dalhousie University and **~2 years of prior industry experience**, the 2027 tech job landscape in Canada – and especially Atlantic Canada – looks promising. Your profile would likely be considered **early-career but not entry-level**, which is advantageous given the trends. Many tech employers seek candidates with **2-5 years experience for intermediate roles**, and indeed **just over half of tech job postings specify a work-experience requirement【1†L135-L143】**. With two years at a major IT firm (Infosys) plus an advanced degree, you would meet these requirements for a large number of positions. In other words, by 2027 you can **target mid-level positions** rather than pure entry-level jobs.

**In-Demand Roles for Your Skill Level:** Considering your background in systems engineering and advanced computing, you could pursue roles such as:  
- **Software Developer/Engineer** – Continues to be one of the *most in-demand occupations* in Canada and Atlantic Canada【8†L169-L177】. Nova Scotia, for example, rates *“Computer systems developers and programmers”* as a **Very Good** prospect occupation through 2026【6†L97-L105】, and *software developers/programmers* are a large and growing group in the region. With your coding and systems experience, you’d fit well here.  
- **Systems Analyst or Business Systems Specialist** – Bridging IT and business, these roles are also in growth mode (business systems specialists are forecasted to grow +113% over the decade nationally)【19†L81-L89】. Atlantic Canada’s outlook lists *Information systems specialists* among fast-growing jobs (900+ openings in NS by 2026)【6†L87-L95】. Your Infosys background likely gave exposure to large systems, which is valuable.  
- **Data Analyst or Data Scientist** – If your MACS program includes data science or AI, these roles are booming. Data-related roles have triple-digit growth forecasts nationally【19†L81-L89】. Even regionally, organizations in Halifax (finance, ocean research, health analytics) seek data professionals. A master’s degree would be a strong asset here.  
- **Cybersecurity Specialist** – Given New Brunswick’s cyber cluster and investments in Nova Scotia, security analysts are in demand. With some upskilling (certifications or courses during your master’s), you could transition into a security engineering or analyst role if interested. Cyber roles are expected to grow swiftly (+131% nationally in a decade)【19†L83-L87】 and are rated “Good” prospects in NS【6†L101-L109】.  
- **IT Consultant or Project Manager** – With a couple years of experience, you might also look at consulting firms or tech service companies in Atlantic Canada. Many are looking for people who can manage projects or liaise with clients (especially as Atlantic Canada’s IT service sector is 32% of the professional/tech workforce【8†L102-L110】). An advanced degree could help you fast-track into these roles.

**Job Levels and Progression:** By 2027, you would present as a **mid-level professional (e.g. “Intermediate Software Engineer” or “Systems Analyst II”)** rather than a fresh grad. Employers in Canada typically recognize international experience from reputable firms, so your two years at Infosys will be valued. You might find yourself competing with other early-career professionals, but remember that the **talent shortage in tech gives you leverage** – Atlantic employers are eager for qualified candidates. Companies may offer relocation packages or remote options to attract candidates like you, since **smaller markets are looking to draw talent from larger centers【19†L121-L129】**. With a Dalhousie degree (well-regarded in the region) and practical experience, you should have a **good chance at “Good” or “Very Good” outlook jobs** in Atlantic Canada【6†L97-L105】【6†L119-L123】. The **median salaries** for these intermediate tech roles in Atlantic Canada are also appealing – for example, a software developer in Halifax was earning around **$76,000 USD (~$100,000 CAD) on average in 2023**【13†L89-L97】, and wages have been rising ~5–10% over 5 years. While actual offers in 2027 will vary, tech wages are expected to remain well above the norm【19†L127-L135】, reflecting the high demand.

**Conclusion:** In summary, Canada’s tech industry is on a healthy growth path heading into 2027, with **national trends favoring continued job creation and innovation**. The Atlantic provinces, particularly Nova Scotia, are capitalizing on these trends and **building vibrant tech ecosystems** – meaning more opportunities close to home for graduates like you. Both the data and the qualitative signals suggest a positive outlook: **more jobs, new specialties (AI, data, cyber), and rising demand for educated tech workers**. With your Master’s and experience, you’ll be entering the job market at an opportune time, equipped to take on an intermediate role in Canada’s evolving tech landscape. Keeping your skills updated (e.g. AI literacy, cloud, agile methodologies) will further boost your prospects, as the industry is ever-changing. Overall, by early 2027 you can expect a **favorable job landscape** – one where your qualifications are highly valued and where tech expertise remains a cornerstone of economic growth in Canada【15†L121-L129】【10†L289-L297】.
