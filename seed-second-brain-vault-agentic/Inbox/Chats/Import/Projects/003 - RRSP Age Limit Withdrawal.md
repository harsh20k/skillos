# RRSP Age Limit Withdrawal

**Priority:** Medium

## Conversation

### Assistant

Below is a **clear, practical way to approach RRSPs** given your situation.

---

## Your situation (important facts)
- Born **1992** → **32–33 years old**
- **Arrived in Canada this year** → **new tax resident**
- No prior Canadian income history

---

## Key rule that affects you immediately
You **cannot contribute to an RRSP until you have Canadian earned income and file a tax return**.

RRSP contribution room is created as:
> **18% of your previous year’s Canadian earned income**  
(up to the annual cap)

Since this is your **first year in Canada**:
- ❌ No RRSP room **this year**
- ✅ RRSP room starts **after you file your first Canadian tax return**

---

## Step-by-step approach (recommended)

### Step 1: Focus on TFSA first (this year)
As a new resident, you **do get TFSA room** starting the year you become a tax resident.

- 2025 TFSA limit: **$7,000**
- TFSA withdrawals are **completely tax-free**
- Much better than RRSP for newcomers

**Action:**  
Open a TFSA and use it for savings or investing now.

---

### Step 2: Work + file your first Canadian tax return
Once you:
- Earn Canadian income (job, TA, internship, etc.)
- File your tax return (spring next year)

You will then receive **RRSP room** shown on your Notice of Assessment.

---

### Step 3: Start RRSP from year 2 onward
RRSP becomes useful when:
- Your income is **moderate to high**
- You want to **reduce taxable income**
- You plan to stay in Canada long term

For most newcomers:
- TFSA first
- RRSP later

---

## RRSP vs TFSA (simple rule)
| Situation | Better choice |
|---------|--------------|
| New to Canada | **TFSA** |
| Low income | **TFSA** |
| High income | **RRSP** |
| Short-term needs | **TFSA** |
| Long-term retirement | **RRSP + TFSA** |

---

## What NOT to do
- ❌ Do not rush into RRSP contributions this year
- ❌ Do not assume you have RRSP room automatically
- ❌ Do not withdraw RRSP early once you start (tax hit)
